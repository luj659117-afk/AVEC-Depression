# Abstract

Depression is a prevalent and severe global mental disorder, yet its diagnosis and treatment encounter numerous challenges. This study introduces an efficient depression identification network, termed DNet. Our approach utilizes facial images and local facial images as crucial sources of data. Although people with depression may exhibit similar general facial expressions, subtle changes in specific facial areas (e.g., eyes, mouth) can be observed as the severity of depression increases. These changes are reflected in the models’ attention mechanisms, highlighting the differences between severity levels. To achieve higher recognition accuracy, a method is required to fuse advanced semantic features between local and global features. Therefore, we propose DNet, comprising two key components: the Feature Extraction Module (FEM) and the Vision Transformer (ViT) Block. Specifically, FEM introduces an attention mechanism that considers both channel and positional information of the feature Map. Two FEMs are employed to separately process facial and local facial images, extracting critical features to generate highly semantic information-rich feature Maps. Subsequently, the feature maps of both images are concatenated along the channel dimension, Using FPN feature fusion, and the ViT Block is utilized to comprehensively learn advanced semantic features of local and global information related to different facial expression regions. Finally, a $1 \times 1$ convolution layer and a fully connected layer are applied to adjust feature channels, yielding more robust predictive results and ultimately outputting depression prediction scores.We experimentally validate the DNet network on the AVEC2014 dataset and our self-constructed CZ2023 dataset, obtaining results of ${ \mathsf { M A E } } = 6 . 0 9 ,$ RMSE = 7.85, and MAE $= 6 . 7 3$ , RMSE $= 8 . 4 7$ , respectively. These results affirm the effectiveness of the proposed method.

# Introduction

Depression is a prevalent and severe global mental disorder, with the worldwide number of affected individuals surpassing 350 million [1] and exhibiting a persistent up- ward trend. Its symptoms not only encompass a low mood but also involve the loss of interest or pleasure [1]. Beyond emotional manifestations, depression influences non-verbal expressions in individuals, encompassing changes in sleep patterns, appetite, energy levels, attention, and self-image, and may even lead to suicidal tendencies. Those affected by depression often exhibit constrained facial expressions [2], primarily evident in the movement of the eyes, eyebrows, mouth, and corners of the mouth, such as a lack of smiles and eye contact. The frequency and intensity of head movements are also significantly reduced [3]. 

In recent years, depression has garnered widespread societal attention; however, its diagnosis and treatment con- tinue to face numerous challenges, including social stigma, exorbitant treatment costs, and issues related to treatment accessibility. Early detection and diagnosis are crucial for assisting patients in accessing effective treatment. If individuals with depression can be identified promptly, they can benefit from interventions such as pharmacotherapy, psychotherapy, and other clinical approaches. Nevertheless, diagnosing depression poses challenges due to its diverse manifestations, and diagnostic methods primarily rely on patient self-reporting and the subjective judgment of clinical practitioners [4]. 

The severity of depression is typically determined through self-reported questionnaires, such as the Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II) [5]. However, relevant studies [6] indicate that clinical practitioners face challenges in iden- tifying depression, and the results of subjective assessment methods often exhibit inconsistency [7]. Achieving a rel- atively objective assessment may require the involvement of multiple clinical experts. This has led to the inefficiency of traditional manual depression detection, which is prone to generating numerous false alarms and may even result in severe consequences, including patient fatalities. Hence, there is an urgent need to develop an easily accessible and privacy-preserving automated detection method to enhance the accuracy of depression diagnosis and treatment out- comes, while providing timely support and intervention for patients. 

With the increasing number of individuals affected by depression, early diagnosis and the periodic reassessment of treatment effectiveness often face limitations and time- consuming challenges. Therefore, Machine Learning-based Automatic Depression Diagnosis (ADD) has emerged. Au- tomatic depression diagnosis aims to identify depression more objectively, reliably, and automatically by analyzing physiological or behavioral signals 

of patients. Non-verbal cues, such as linguistic prompts [8–13] and. 

facial expressions [14–17], are widely employed for predicting the severity of depression. Simultaneously, the collection of various modal signals from participants, in- cluding electroencephalogram (EEG) [18, 19], near-infrared. 

spectroscopy [20, 21], brain imaging [22, 23], and eyetracking data [24], holds significant importance in the anal- ysis of depression. 

Current research predominantly focuses on facial infor- mation due to its convenient acquisition, noncontact ad- vantages, and the fact that facial and head gestures account for over half of human activities [2, 14, 17, 24]. However, existing manual feature extraction methods have not fully leveraged color information [15, 25, 26]. To address this, researchers have proposed some enhanced methods, such as using quaternion algorithms to extract Local Binary Pat- tern (LBP) features [27–29], embedding color infor- mation into quaternion representations. Additionally, deep learning methods have made significant strides in automatic depression diagnosis. Numerous studies have utilized con- volutional neural networks to extract deep features from visual training data and capture facial landmark informa- tion [30–33]. These semantic features demonstrate greater discriminative capabilities in automatic depression diagnosis compared to traditional manual features. However, the limited amount of depression data available for deep representation learning and the challenges posed by large model parameters for practical application have hindered the further development of deep learning in automatic depres- sion diagnosis. 

In order to advance the field of automatic depression diagnosis, this paper introduces a lightweight but effective design named DNet, which utilizes only facial image data to automatically predict scores on the Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II) and the Hamilton Depression Rating Scale (HMAD). The second visual information focuses on local facial regions, including eyebrows, eyes, nose, and mouth. # Related work

In recent years, there has been a growing emphasis in the field of automatic depression diagnosis on leveraging behavioral signals such as speech and visual cues. The Depression Sub-Challenge of the Audio-Visual Emotion Challenge has spurred rapid advancements in deep learning methods for automatic depression recognition. These stud- ies predominantly utilized the AVEC2013 and AVEC2014. 

databases, introducing regression methods for depression prediction. The approaches can be categorized into three groups: those based on audio data, video data, and multi- modal data. Despite the superior performance of multimodal approaches in experimental results, this study, considering the convenience of data collection and 

recognition accuracy, remains dedicated to the automatic recognition of depression based on visual signals. 

# Deep ADD networks for audio modality

The audio modality plays a crucial role in depression recognition. Below is a detailed description of feature ex- traction from audio cues for predicting the severity of de- pression.In 2016, Ma et al. [13] introduced a deep learning model named DepAudioNet. This model incorporates Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) and Deep Convolutional Neu- ral Network (DCNN) to encode audio features, extracting representations of depression from speech cues for depres- sion recognition. During the model training phase, they introduced a random sampling strategy to balance positive and negative samples, significantly alleviating the bias issue caused by uneven sample distribution. By extracting high- level, short-term, and long-term features, the model can effectively distinguish between the healthy control group and individuals with depression. 

In 2018, He et al. [35] proposed an approach that com- bines deep learning and handcrafted features for assessing the severity of depression in speech. They extracted hand- crafted features of the audio (LLD) and handcrafted features of the spectrogram (MRELBP). These features were then input into a Deep Convolutional Neural Network (DCNN) to obtain deep learning features from the original audio and spectrogram. Subsequently, they employed a joint fine- tuning technique to learn complementary representations between handcrafted and deep learning features, introducing data augmentation methods to address the issue of insuffi- cient samples. 

To address the limited scale of depression recognition databases, Yang et al. [36] introduced the use of Deep Convolutional Generative Adversarial Networks (DCGAN) to augment audio signal data samples, thereby enhancing accuracy. In a recent study [37], the authors proposed a deep architecture for automatic depression recognition from speech. They fused Speaker Recognition (SR) and Speech Emotion Recognition (SER) features, leveraging comple- mentary information between human speech and emotion differences to improve depression recognition performance while mitigating overfitting issues. Additionally, they intro- duced a feature variation coherence measurement algorithm to model the correlation and covariance coefficients of delayed multi-channel variations. 

# Deep ADD networks for video modality

In addition to audio information, visual information is equally crucial in automatic depression recognition, offering the advantages of easy acquisition and non-contact interac- tion. The following are some relevant studies 

on depression recognition based on visual information. Song et al. [26] proposed a multi-scale architecture that uses human behavioral primitives (AUs, gaze direction, and head pose) as frame-wise feature representations. They then employed spectral heatmaps and spectral vectors to ex- tract multi-scale representations of behavior. Deep Con- volutional Neural Networks (DCNN) and Artificial Neu- ral Networks (ANN) were utilized for depression recogni- tion, achieving good performance on the AVEC2013 and AVEC2014 datasets. DE MELO et al. [38] introduced a novel 3D CNN called Multi-Scale Spatio-Temporal Network (MSN) for learning facial feature information in video seg- ments. MSN employs multiple parallel convolutional layers to capture spatial and temporal changes, particularly those related to facial expressions. They also proposed the Maxi- mally Divergent Network (MDN) [39] for representing facial expression changes relevant to depression assessment. These methods demonstrated advanced results on the AVEC2013 and AVEC2014 datasets. 

Uddin et al. [40] employed Bi-directional Long Short-Term Memory (Bi-LSTM) for depression recognition. They combined the Inception-ResNet-v2 network structure with Volume Local Directional Number (VLDN) to extract fa- cial features. These features were input into multi-layer Bi- LSTM, and the depression scores were output through fusion at the decision layer. This method exhibited good perfor- mance on the AVEC2013 and AVEC2014 datasets. Addi- tionally, He et al. [41] proposed a comprehensive framework called Deep Local-Global Attention Convolutional Neural Network (DLGA-CNN). This framework integrates CNN with attention mechanisms and Weighted Spatial Pyramid Pooling (WSPP) to learn both deep and global representations. They used Local Attention CNN (LA-CNN) and Global Attention CNN (GA-CNN) to focus on local image blocks and global patterns of the entire facial region, introducing a Local-Global Attention CNN (LGA-CNN). Potential depression patterns were learned through feature aggregation and the WSPP method. 

Recently, Niu et al. [42] introduced dual attention mod- ules and element-wise calibration modules to capture subtle depression signals. These modules, through bilinear atten- tion, highlight feature components crucial for depression, and embed global information into feature tensors to further emphasize important elements, thereby enhancing depres- sion prediction capabilities. 

# Deep ADD networks for Multi-modal fusion

Methods that fuse information from multiple modalities can enhance the performance of depression prediction, espe- cially when combining audio and visual data. Here are some studies on multimodal fusion approaches for depression recognition. 

Syed et al. [43] proposed a method for recognizing bipolar affective disorder using traditional machine learning approaches. They introduced turbulence features to capture sudden and unstable changes in feature profiles from audio and visual modalities. Additionally, they employed Fisher Vector encoding of ComParE lowlevel descriptors (LLDs). 

to fuse multimodal information. In 2017, Yang et al. [44] presented a combined approach using Deep Convolutional Neural Network (DCNN) and Deep Neural Network (DNN) methods. They fused audio, video, and text inputs into a single framework for depression prediction. This method exhibited competitive performance in the AVEC2017 challenge. 

Niu et al. [45] proposed a method that combines Spatio- Temporal Attention (STA) network and Multimodal Attention Feature Fusion (MAFF). They first segmented audio and video data into fixed-length segments and extracted features using the STA network. Subsequently, they employed MAFF to generate multimodal representations for estimating the severity of depression. Yang et al. [46] introduced an ensemble learning strategy on the AVEC2018 dataset, combining DNN and random forest for bipolar depression analysis. They utilized ensemble learning to mitigate over- fitting issues, particularly considering the limited training data. Zhang et al. [47] first used DNN (Deep Neural Network) to learn deep features of audio, video and text modalities, and then integrated the deep features learned from different modalities through a feature fusion strategy. 

While many methods have utilized Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to extract data containing depression- related information, the relationship between local facial regions and the overall facial representation when leveraging visual information is often overlooked. Previous research has predominantly employed the approach of dividing the origi- nal facial image into multiple regions to extract local features [41, 48, 49]. However, these methods have limitations as they restrict the model’s ability to detect depression clues in spatial distribution, thereby hindering further improvement in depression recognition performance. 

In this study, we opt to capture multiple facial regions on feature maps and construct a comprehensive automatic depression severity prediction system through aspects like feature extraction, fusion, and concatenation. Compared to the majority of existing methods, our proposed approach demonstrates superior performance in predicting depression severity. 

# Large Language models in depression recognition

In recent years, as large language models (LLMs) have become increasingly capable of understanding complex texts, researchers have begun to explore their potential in 

the field of mental health, especially in depression recognition. Xu et al. conducted a comprehensive evaluation of several mainstream LLMs [50], including GPT-3.5, FLAN- T5, and Alpaca, on various mental health prediction tasks using online text data. Their experiments covered zero-shot prompting, few-shot prompting, and instruction fine-tuning. The results demonstrate that instruction-tuned models, par- ticularly their proposed Mental-Alpaca and Mental-FLAN- T5, significantly outperform much larger models such as GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 with prompt-based approaches. This highlights the effectiveness of domain-specific fine-tuning in enhancing LLM performance for mental health assessment tasks. 

Another direction worth paying attention to is the integration of large language models and physiological signals. Hu et al. explored the integration of large language models (LLMs) with physiological signals such as EEG for mental health assessment [51]. Instead of relying on a single modal- ity, their approach combines EEG, facial expressions, and textual/audio inputs in a multimodal framework, enabling LLMs to process and reason over Heterogeneous signals. The study finds that such multimodal combinations substantially outperform unimodal baselines, and that 1-shot prompting yields better results than zero-shot, highlighting the potential of LLMs in modeling low-resource multimodal data for psychological state classification. 

In addition, Abdelrahaman et al. investigated a comprehensive multimodal framework that fuses text, audio, and video for depression detection, with a novel integration of LLM Prediction as part of decision-layer fusion [52]. They first preprocessed the text and audio at the speech unit level (chunking) and used a variety of pre-trained embedding models to generate representations; CNN and BiLSTM were used to extract facial expressions and dynamic features for video frames. Subsequently, different fusion experiments were conducted at the data layer, feature layer, and decision layer, in which LLM prediction output for text emotions was added to the decision layer. The results show that speech unit-level chunking significantly improves performance on text and audio, and after the decision layer integrates LLM predictions, it achieves a balanced accuracy of $9 4 . 8 \%$ in depression detection, which is better than pure vision or pure text, and multimodal solutions without LLM. 

Overall, recent work illustrates that LLMs not only excel at text-based depression recognition and signal interpreta- tion, but when fused with facial expression features they can more accurately capture multimodal depression biomarkers. Future research should explore tighter integration of patient clinical history, longitudinal visual dynamics, and person- alized prompting strategies to further enhance LLM-based decision support in realworld mental health care. 

# Methods and material

This section will elaborate on the structural components of the DNet model, which comprises two pivotal modules: the Feature Extraction Module (FEM) and the ViT Block. The primary objective of DNet is to accurately predict scores on depression-related scales using facial images. The specific steps of our methodology are delineated as fol- lows: Firstly, we employ the Multi-Task Cascaded Convolutional Network (MTCNN) to crop facial images from each video frame, concurrently utilizing OpenCV’s affine trans- formation technique for facial alignment. Subsequently, two FEM modules are dedicated to processing both global and local facial images, extracting key features, and generating feature Maps with highly semantic information. The feature Maps of these two images are then concatenated along the channel dimension. Input to FPN for feature fusion. The ViT Block is employed to comprehensively learn high-level se- mantic features of both local and global contexts, capturing information from different facial expression regions. Lastly, fine-tuning of feature channels is accomplished by applying a $1 \times 1$ convolutional layer and a fully connected layer, ensuring more robust predictive outcomes, ultimately yielding the depression prediction scores. The overall structure of Dnet is shown in Fig. 1.Subsequently, we will engage in a detailed discussion of each component within the DNet architecture. 

# Feature extraction module

Traditional Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have been widely employed for modeling high-level semantic structures in data relevant to specific visual tasks. In gen- eral, CNNs are designed to facilitate deep feature learning by sequentially processing data, extracting features from low to high-level nonlinear representations. In this deep architecture, multiple layers of data processing operations, such as convolution and pooling, are concatenated to reveal prominent signals at different scales within the images.To extract global facial features from facial images, we have constructed the Feature Extraction Module (FEM). The FEM is composed of a multitude of stacked Conv Blocks and In Blocks, as illustrated in Fig. 1.And they are connected in series with DenseBlock to enhance the feature reuse capability of the network, and FPN is added to fuse the output of each convolutional layer. FPN collects the features of the output of four convolutional layers in the network (that is, the results after four layers of convolution), inputs these features into FPN for fusion, and finally outputs a fused feature map. In contrast to traditional residual structures, the proposed Conv Block and In Block in this paper take into consideration both channel information and directionally relevant positional information of the feature maps. 

This enhances the expressive capacity for feature representation, effectively regulates the learning process, and prevents over- fitting, thereby contributing to the acceleration of network convergence. And the features after FPN fusion contain multi-level information, which helps to improve the performance of the network. The following sections will provide a detailed exposition of the Conv Block, In Block and specific settings of Denseblock and FPN in this article. 

# Conv block

The proposed Conv Block structure in this paper is depicted in Fig. 2, comprising two fundamental $3 \times \ 3$ convolutional layers and a Coordinate Attention module. Assuming the input feature map size is $( C - 3 2 ) \times 2 H \times 2 W$ , where $C$ represents the number of channels in the feature map, and $H$ and $W$ denote the height and width of the feature map, respectively. Each pair of convolutional layers is interspersed with a batch normalization layer and ReLU activation function [53]. 

In the first convolutional layer of the initial Residual Block, the channel count of the feature Map is increased by 32, while simultaneously halving the feature map’s size. To ensure that the output of the first Residual Block has the same feature map size as the input when adding them together, we apply a $1 \times \ 1$ convolutional layer to the input data. This operation achieves an increase in channel count and a halving of the feature map size, resulting in the output feature map with dimensions $C \times H \times W$ . The computational process is expressed by the following formula: 

$$
x c = W 2 \sigma (W 1 * x) \tag {1}
$$

In the above formulas, $x$ represents the input features of the Residual Block, and $x c$ represents the features to be input to the Coordinate Attention (CA) mechanism. In the equa- tions, $W _ { 1 }$ and $W _ { 2 }$ represent the convolutional parameters to. 

be learned, and $\sigma$ denotes the ???????? non-linear activation function. 

In the Coordinate Attention (CA) mechanism, to avoid compressing all spatial information into channels, we perform global average pooling separately in both the width and height directions on the input feature map. This is done to obtain feature maps in the width and height directions, aiding in acquiring more accurate spatial positional information. For the input feature map of size $C \times H \times W$ , we conduct average pooling in the $X$ and $Y$ directions, resulting in a feature map of size $C \times H \times 1$ in the height dimension and $C \times 1 \times W$ in the width dimension. The specific calculations are outlined in formulas 2 and 3.

# ViT block

While individuals may exhibit similar depression scores, significant variations persist in their facial expressions. Moreover, subtle and intricate facial expression changes exist among individuals with varying levels of depression. When processing a given facial image, not all facial regions contribute equally to the detection of depression. Therefore, in many instances, it is imperative to focus on specific regions to capture local features associated with depression. To address this need, the present study introduces the ViT Block to learn both local and global representations of ad- vanced semantic features extracted by two Facial Expression Modules (FEMs). This integrated design allows the model to attend to distinct local regions, thereby better capturing subtle variations and crucial information in the image, enhancing the model’s sensitivity to varying degrees of depression emotions. Consequently, this design effectively handles both local and global features, providing more accurate information for depression identification. 

The structure of the ViT Block is illustrated in Fig. 5. Initially, local features are modeled using a $3 \times 3$ convolutional layer, followed by an adjustment of channel numbers through a $1 \times 1$ convolutional layer. Subsequently, the feature map undergoes global feature modeling through the Unfold ->Transformer ->Fold structure. Unfold ->Trans- former ->Fold only selects pixels with the same position for dot product operation in the self-attention mechanism. This is because performing self-attention operation with all pixels may introduce information redundancy, and not all pixels contain useful information. # AVEC2014

The AVEC2014 dataset is a subset of the AVEC2013 dataset, curated by filtering based on the Audio-Visual Emo- tion Challenge and Workshop (AVEC) depression Language corpus. This subset is specifically tailored for research focused on the study of depressive emotions. The dataset comprises 300 video segments, with durations ranging from 6 s to 4 min. In total, 84 participants took part, with an average age of 31.5 years, a standard deviation of 12.3 years, and an age range spanning from 18 to 63 years. The dataset exhibits a similar distribution in terms of age, gender, and depression levels. Some participants appear in multiple video segments, with each participant recording between 1 and 4 sessions, separated by two weeks between each recording. Among these, 18 participants engaged in 3 recordings, 31 participants in 2 recordings, and 34 participants in 1 recording. Each participant recorded under two different human-computer interaction tasks, employing a webcam and microphone in quiet environments. These tasks include:1. Northwind Task: Participants read a passage from the fable “Die Sonne und der Wind” (The North Wind and the Sun) in German.2. Freeform Task: Participants answered one of a series of questions, such as “What is your favorite dish?” or discussed a poignant childhood memory in German. 

In the AVEC2014 dataset, the recorded videos for the Northwind and Freeform tasks are treated as independent samples. This is because each participant contributes two videos with the expectation that they exhibit the same level of depression. Each video file comes with annotations indicating the severity of depression based on the Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II). The BDI-II scores range from 0 to 63, where scores of 0 to 10 

represent a normal state, 11 to 16 indicate mild mood disturbance, 17 to 20 suggest clinical depression threshold, 21 to 30 denote moderate depression, 31 to 40 signify severe depression, and scores above 40 indicate extreme depression. In the AVEC2014 dataset, the highest annotated score is 45, indicating that the dataset includes participants with depression severity reaching extreme levels. 

The AVEC2014 dataset is divided into training, validation, and test sets, each containing 100 video segments. The videos for the “Northwind” task and the “Freeform” task are evenly distributed within each set, with 50 videos for each task. In the training set, the average BDI-II score is 15.0. In the development set, the average BDI-II scores for the “Northwind” and “Freeform” tasks are 15.6 (with standard deviations of 12.3 and 12.0, respectively). During experimentation, the training set combines data from both “Northwind” and “Freeform” tasks for model training. Subsequently, the development set is utilized to validate the model’s performance, and finally, the test set is employed to assess the model.

In comparison to the top-performing ResNet-34 [60] in the ResNet series, DNet reduces RMSE by 1.36 and MaE by 0.855, indicating supe- rior performance in network structure compared to the resid- ual architecture. Additionally, we conducted comparative experiments with some classic lightweight networks. Com- pared to GoogLeNet 

[61] with a multi-scale convolutional structure, DNet reduces RMSE by 1.31 and MaE by 0.795. In comparison to the streamlined lightweight SqueezeNet [62], DNet decreases RMSE by 1.34 and MaE by 0.855. Even when compared to the best-performing lightweight network, MobileNetV3 [63], DNet lowers RMSE by 0.92 and MaE by 0.545. Compared with the ViT model with relatively large parameters, DNet can reduce RMSE and MaE by 0.83 and 0.555 respectively. This shows that DNet not only has excellent performance, but also can be more easily deployed and run in resource-limited environments. 

# Inference efficiency and deployment feasibility

To further validate the deployment potential of DNet, we conducted an inference performance evaluation. The experiment was performed on an NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU with batch size set to 1, simulating the real-time inference scenario of edge deployment. Results show that the average inference time per image is $2 8 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m s }$ , and the Maximum GPU memory usage is 239.9 MB. 

These results demonstrate that DNet achieves not only low parameter count (20.7 M) and FLOPs (0.064 GFLOPs), but also practical runtime efficiency and memory econ- omy, which are critical for real-world deployment. While these tests were conducted on a desktop GPU, they provide an empirical basis supporting DNet’s deployability in low-resource environments. In future work, we plan to validate inference performance on 

embedded platforms such as Jet- son Nano or mobile CPUs.

# Error analysis

To facilitate the effective application of DNet in clinical practice, we conducted an in-depth analysis of the errors for each test sample in the AVEC2014 and CZ2023 datasets, presenting the results in Fig. 9, ordered by increasing error. For the AVEC2014 dataset, the analysis reveals that error probabilities are approximately uniformly distributed between 0 and 13, with only a very small number of samples exceeding this range. Over $6 0 \%$ of the samples have model errors below 6.0. On the CZ2023 dataset, the error distribution primarily falls between 0 and 15, with only 12 samples exceeding this range. Over $5 0 \%$ of the samples exhibit model errors below 6.0. An error of approximately 6.0 indi- cates misclassifications occurring Mainly between adjacent depression severity categories or at category boundaries. The worst-case scenario is 

when a subject with the lowest depression level is misclassified as severe, occurring when the error exceeds 16. In both the AVEC2014 and CZ2023 datasets, our model produces errors greater than 16 for only 6 and 8 videos, respectively. These results indicate that DNet possesses robust generalization capabilities, with a low prob- ability of severe misclassifications. This contributes to the practical application of the DNet model in clinical settings, offering accurate depression identification and assessment. 

Through a comprehensive series of comparative experiments, we have validated the exceptional performance of the DNet model in the task of automatic depression recognition when using only facial images as input data. DNet demon- strates the ability to effectively learn rich facial expression representations related to depression with limited training data. Moreover, it achieves this with a relatively small num- ber of model parameters and efficient computational capa- bilities, showcasing substantial potential for future clinical applications. 

# Conclusions

This paper introduces a deployment-friendly and efficient network named DNet, designed to extract features highly correlated with depression symptoms from facial images. DNet comprises two key components: FEM and ViT Block. Firstly, DNet utilizes two FEMs to extract latent features from both full-face and local facial images. The combination of these two components Helps leverage complementary information from different facial regions, enhancing the overall recognition performance compared to deep models with a single data input. Then use FPN for feature fusion to extract more advanced and rich features. Secondly, the paper introduces the ViT Block to learn local and global high- level semantic features, capturing information from different facial regions. Finally, through a $1 \times 1$ convolutional layer and a fully connected layer to adjust feature channels, DNet outputs the predicted depression scores. The proposed model is evaluated on two depression datasets, AVEC2014 and CZ2023, with experimental results clearly demonstrating the excellence of our proposed model. Furthermore, we have empirically verified DNet’s inference speed and memory efficiency under realistic settings, providing further support for its suitability in deployment-oriented applications. In the future, we plan to study depression recognition networks based on video stream data, adopt more complex network structures and feature extraction techniques to further improve the recognition performance of depression, and try to reduce the number of model parameters. In order to cope with complex depression detection scenarios.
